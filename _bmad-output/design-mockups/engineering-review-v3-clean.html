From Senior to Staff

What you need to change — and what your organization actually needs to provide. The jump fails for two reasons and most advice only covers one.

By Robert · Engineering Excellence
career-growth staff-engineer promotion

Most "how to get to staff" articles give you one side of the story — think bigger, work across teams, drive strategy. That's all true, but it's only half of it. Half the story is worse than no story, because it lets you blame yourself for things that aren't your fault.

Getting to staff is a two-player game. You need to change how you work. And your company needs to actually have a real staff role with real work attached to it. If either side isn't holding up their end, you're stuck — and I've watched talented engineers burn years chasing a title that didn't actually exist at their company.

01What you need to change

The behavioral shifts are well-trodden ground. You stop measuring your value by lines of code and start measuring it by problems eliminated. You stop being the smartest person in the room and start making the room smarter. You learn to write — properly, with structure, because written artifacts are how staff engineers scale.

The senior engineer's job is to do the work well. The staff engineer's job is to make sure the right work gets done.

The honest part is that this transition is uncomfortable, and most of the discomfort comes from giving up the things that made you good at being senior. The fast hands. The deep flow. The satisfying close of a hard problem on Friday afternoon. Staff work is slower, more diffuse, and most of your wins look like things not happening — a migration that didn't go sideways, a contract that didn't get signed, a team that didn't burn out.

02What your org must provide

The other half: there has to be staff-level work available. Not staff-level headcount — actual problems that require a staff engineer to solve.
