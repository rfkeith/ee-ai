## Sharpened thesis (one sentence)

Capability arrives before the self-image does — you can be doing the job well before you believe you're the person who does it — and the standard "believe in yourself first" advice fails because a self-image can't be argued into existence, only *experienced* into it; sometimes the role has to be handed to you before the belief can catch up.

## Shape

Default argument shape (thesis → conventional take → where it breaks → the other half → grounded close), with the heavy "other half" split into *mechanism* + *two paths* (the IC who's stuck; the leader who can hand someone the role).

## Where the human shows up — and the flag

This thesis is abstraction-prone; "self-image" can float off into self-help vapor. It's anchored by the author's scar in two places — the in-medias-res open (§1) and the turn (§4). **If those two land, the essay is trustworthy; if they're thin, it reads as a Maltz book report.** Guard them.

## Section talking points

Considerations to structure your thinking as you draft. *Prompts, not prose* — answer them in your head (or in margin notes), then write the section.

### §1 — Open (in medias res)

*Scene chosen: the contractor-fee line to a senior engineer (see Sections table).*

- The contractor-fee line is the load-bearing dialogue — render it close to verbatim. Paraphrasing softens the con; the exact mercenary phrasing is what makes the brain's lie audible to the reader.
- Show enough of the senior engineer to make them human — one detail of who they are or what they were deciding — without turning them into a character. They're the witness, not the subject.
- Hold back the *significance* of the moment ("first time I sensed I was holding someone accountable"). §1 *shows* it; §2 *names* it. If you explain it inside §1, you've collapsed two sections into one and killed the thesis-after-scene structure.
- Resist any reference to *Stop Solving, Start Shaping* or Senior→Staff here. That callback belongs in §2 and lands harder if §1 doesn't pre-empt it. The reader should feel the resonance only when you make it in the next section.
- Set a hard length cap (≤150 words is a fair starting point) and hold the comic-tragic register — you were *fooled* by your own framing, not victimised. The contractor-fee joke is darkly funny; let it be funny without winking at the reader.

### §2 — Thesis + the callback

- Decide where the title's "con your brain runs" frame surfaces in the prose. Once, here, as the thesis statement? Or threaded across the essay? Single use is usually stronger.
- Settle on **one** consistent term for the third player — "self-image," "belief," "your brain" — and stick with it. Switching mid-essay dilutes the spine.
- Workshop the thesis until it fits on one breath. The title earned a sharp thesis; don't blunt it with hedges.
- The Senior→Staff callback: decide the dose. Direct quote of the "two-player game" frame? Paraphrase? Or a one-line link and move on? Don't re-litigate the prior piece.
- Trap: don't tease the scar here. §1 showed it; §4 lands it. This section is *argument*, not more story.

### §3 — The conventional take, stated fairly

- List 2-3 specific versions of the advice you've actually encountered ("fake it till you make it," "act as if," "visualise yourself in the role"). Don't compose a generic version — that's a strawman.
- Identify the *single* premise the advice rests on (belief precedes capability). Naming it here lets §4-5 attack the premise, not the advice in general.
- Acknowledge sincerely where the advice *does* work. The reader has to nod here, or §4's turn won't land.
- No naming names. Don't cite specific authors or influencers — that's punching down, and you don't need them as opponents to make the argument.
- Word-count check: this is a setup section, not the heart. If it runs longer than §4, you've over-corrected for fairness.

### §4 — Where it breaks — the turn (the scar)

*Scene chosen: the VP-promotion forum (see Sections table).*

- The scene's centre of gravity is the promotion forum. Set it up in two beats: a *brief* nod to the baptism-of-fire period (you took the role, you had 15 reports cold) and then drop the reader into the forum itself. Don't linger on the months between — the realisation lives in the moment of advocacy, not the arc to it.
- This scene is the essay's *triumph beat* — but the register should stay matter-of-fact, not dramatic. The power is in the quiet: "I made the case. They accepted it. Oh — I'm the person who does this now." Performed humility or performed swagger both kill it.
- The realisation is *external-triggered, internal-landed*. Show both halves: the external event (the case landing, the promotion confirmed) and the internal flip in a single quiet line. Don't elide either or the mechanism inversion gets fuzzy.
- The mechanism inversion is the sentence the rest of the essay rests on: belief was downstream of the role, not upstream. Workshop it until it's one breath long, and make sure it earns its weight by *naming what just happened* — not gesturing at a generality.
- Confidentiality, hard line: institution category at most ("a major investment bank"), never the employer; the promoted person is a *member of your team*, never named or characterised in a way that could identify them. The scene works as a *type*, not as a real-person identification.
- Pre-empt-and-resist the §7 connection: you'll be tempted to draw the line from "the role was handed to me" → "I now hand the role to others" right here. *Don't.* §4 is the realisation; §7 names the move. Let §7 do its job.

### §5 — The other half — the mechanism

- Introduce Maltz in one sentence — a plastic surgeon who noticed something. Don't biographize.
- Pick the *single* Maltz claim that does the heaviest lift here: positive thinking can't override the self-image; the self-image only changes through *experience*. Quote sparingly — one phrase max.
- Translate Maltz for the engineering reader. He was writing for a 1960s self-help audience; you need to render "self-image" so it lands at an engineer's desk in 2026 without dumbing down.
- Show *why* the conventional advice (§3) fails *on this specific mechanism* — not "it's wrong" but "here's the precise reason it can't work." That's the load-bearing beam.
- Slop trap: this section is most at risk of becoming Maltz exposition. Every Maltz sentence must earn its keep by sharpening the engineering argument, or cut it.

### §6 — The other half — what you do if no one hands you the role

- Bridge cleanly from §5: the mechanism explains why "believe in yourself" fails; this section explains what *does* work on the same mechanism, and why.
- Render Theatre of the Mind in plain language. Don't reach for "visualise success" — that's the cliché Maltz is actually correcting. His specific claim is *re-experiencing real past wins*. Hold that distinction.
- Get ahead of the eye-roll: name the lab evidence Maltz cites (dart-throwing / basketball / synthetic experience) so the engineer reader can't dismiss it as woo. One mention is enough.
- The actionable beat: what does the reader *do* on Monday? **One** specific practice — not a list, not a 21-day plan (even though Maltz uses 21; leave it).
- The takeaway is the *principle of re-experiencing*, not a ritual. This isn't a productivity blog; resist the urge to prescribe.

### §7 — The other half — what leaders do

- Make the audience shift explicit at the section break: §6 was for the stuck IC; §7 is for the manager looking at one.
- Decide what "hand the role" actually means in this essay. Sponsorship? Formal promotion? A stretch assignment carrying a title? A public introduction at the new altitude? Pick the version you mean and be specific.
- Don't dodge the hard editorial call: sometimes you hand the role and it doesn't take. How do you tell when self-image will catch up vs. when capability genuinely isn't there yet? You owe the reader a heuristic, not a hedge.
- Senior→Staff callback: one line on "active sponsorship" + the link, then move on. Don't re-explain the published piece here.
- Voice trap: stay in *first person*, not adviser-mode ("leaders should…"). Talk about decisions *you* have made about *your* reports. The trust doctrine demands the scar even here.

### §8 — Grounded close

- State the honest position plainly: you grew into one role; the next ceiling (MD) is still there. Don't dramatise it — flat delivery is more trustworthy than performed humility.
- MD tee-up: **one sentence, maximum**. It's bait for the Scar Tissue essay, not a resolution for this one. Resist the urge to close the loop.
- Frame the reader's takeaway as a *question* they can answer Monday morning, not a directive. What audit should they run on themselves?
- Consider an echo of the open — does the close call back to the "just the contractor" scene? Powerful if it lands without being cute; skip it if it doesn't.
- Length check: this should be the shortest section in the essay. If it runs long, you're eulogising. Cut.
