## Scene: The hot-swap

**The moment (the author's words, preserved):**
The team hot-swapped code in the running cluster through a rolling restart. The author had been deliberate in the choice of tech and how it fitted the problem — after the swap landed, the team realised there was something more to him than gold-plating the solution. It was a carefully balanced architectural choice that had to be made early; he knew it would give plenty of options later.

**Genre signal:** technical foresight, deliberate tech selection, vindication of an early call by the team's reaction.

**Could anchor:**
- A Best Practices piece on *architectural foresight* — the option-value of early "over-engineered" decisions that earn their keep at the right moment.
- A complement to the Pre-Mortems draft: pre-mortems *imagine failure*; this scene is about *pre-positioning for survival* — the same discipline running the other direction.
- A Best Practices piece on the *gold-plating vs. foresight* distinction — when does early investment look like over-engineering, and when does it pay back?

**Considered and not used:**
- §1 of `outlines/self-image-ceiling.md` — wrong altitude (external recognition, not internal realisation). 2026-05-31.

**Flags before using:**
- The author's framing emphasises team recognition — the resulting essay must not be a victory lap. The interesting thesis is in the *trade-off* (looks-like-gold-plating vs. actually-foresight), not in "I was right."
- Needs a counter-example to be trustworthy: when has the author over-engineered something that *didn't* pay back? Without that, the piece reads as self-congratulation.
- Confidentiality: institution category at most, no naming, no identifying detail about the tech or the system.

## Scene: The MD pushback

**The moment (the author's words, preserved):**
An MD emailed the author with some "constructive" criticism of the system because the author wouldn't do what he wanted. The author pushed back firmly, explaining all the ways the thing the MD was asking for actually hurt his goals rather than furthered them. The MD's reply acknowledged his impulsiveness; the firm-but-polite, educational rebuttal was gratefully received.

**Genre signal:** principled technical pushback on a senior stakeholder, framing-as-leadership, turning a confrontation into mutual understanding.

**Could anchor:**
- A Leadership piece on *managing up by educating, not capitulating* — the move of reframing a senior person's ask in terms of *their own goals*, rather than fighting on the surface request.
- A Career piece on *firmness with senior stakeholders* — when to push back, how to do it without burning capital, what disagreement-without-defiance actually sounds like.
- Possible companion or sibling to [[Scar Tissue]] — that one is about being judged *by* MDs; this is about how the author *speaks to* them. Different beat, same constellation.

**Considered and not used:**
- §1 of `outlines/self-image-ceiling.md` — wrong type of moment (senior-IC pushback, not the leadership self-image gap). 2026-05-31.

**Flags before using:**
- The author got a *gracious response*. Most pushback stories don't end like this. The trustworthy version either generalises carefully from this single success or pairs it with a pushback that *didn't* land — the latter is more honest.
- Don't punch down on the MD. The "impulsiveness acknowledged" line is the trust-building beat — keep it; don't twist it into "I won."
- Confidentiality: institution category at most, no naming, no identifying detail about the MD or the system.
