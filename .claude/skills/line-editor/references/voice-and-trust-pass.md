# Voice & Trust Pass

Judge whether this draft, as written, earns a reader's trust — and whether it sounds like the journal. This is the anti-slop read on finished prose, distinct from the line edit (which is mechanical) and the citation check (which is factual). The full doctrine lives at `{project-root}/.claude/skills/content-planner/references/trust-doctrine.md`; the editing-relevant parts are below.

## The voice

Two failure modes, both fatal — find them in specific sentences, not in the abstract:

- **Preachy.** A line that wins by sneering at other engineers or "most advice" instead of by being more right. Flag it; it's a rewrite, not a take.
- **Safe.** A line so hedged and agreeable it commits to nothing. The journal needs a sharp opinion that still feels honest — if a paragraph would offend no one and surprise no one, it's saying nothing. Push it toward the real position.

## The trust bar

- **Is the lived stake on the page?** The author's first-hand experience — the real mistake, decision, or scar — must be *visible in the prose*, not merely promised in the premise. A piece that explains a concept correctly but shows no sign the author lived it reads as competent and hollow. Point to where the human is present, and where it's missing.
- **Would anything remain an hour after reading?** A frame, a scar, a changed mind. If the draft is polished but nothing stays, name the paragraphs that are filler and the one idea worth building the piece around.
- **Is anyone home?** Does this read as a person with a point of view, or as a competent summary anyone's pipeline could produce? Synthetic authority, recycled advice dressed as insight, and confident sentences that have never touched real work all fail here.

## The hard boundary

Flag, don't fill. Where the lived stake is missing, ask the author for it — "this section needs the production incident you actually lived; what happened?" — and leave the space for them. Do not invent the anecdote, the scar, or the opinion. Generating the human center is precisely the slop the journal exists to refuse, and it would be worse coming from the editor than from a first draft.

## What success looks like

A short, specific list: the preachy lines, the safe lines, the places the lived stake is asserted but not shown, and the verdict — is this draft trustworthy enough to publish, close with the author supplying named pieces, or not yet there? The author leaves knowing exactly which sentences to fix and which gaps only they can fill.
