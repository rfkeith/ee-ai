# Outline an Essay

Take an idea that's cleared the bar — a sharp thesis with a real lived stake — and turn it into a skeleton the author can sit down and write section by section, without wondering what any section is for. This runs after the pressure-test verdict is "write it"; if the idea hasn't been pressure-tested or has no lived angle yet, route there first.

## The spine is the argument

Engineering Excellence essays are argument-driven nonfiction, not stories. The default shape — visible in the published pieces — is:

1. **Thesis up front.** The claim, stated plainly, usually with the tension that makes it worth reading ("getting to staff is a two-player game").
2. **The conventional take, stated fairly.** The popular advice, given its real due — not a strawman. The reader should nod here.
3. **Where it's incomplete.** Not "everyone's wrong" — "this is half the story." Name the conditions the conventional take ignores, the cases where it breaks.
4. **The other half.** The essay's actual contribution: the framework, the distinction, the missing variable. This is the heaviest section.
5. **A grounded close.** What to do with this — honest, specific, no triumphalism.

This is a default, not a cage. Some pieces are a checklist, a decision framework, or an activity-by-activity breakdown. Pick the shape the argument actually wants and say why.

## Thread the lived stake through it

The argument is the spine; the author's first-hand experience is what makes it trustworthy (see `references/trust-doctrine.md`). Decide where the scar lives and mark it in the outline — usually one of:

- **An in-medias-res open:** drop the reader into the real incident before stating the thesis, then pull back to the claim.
- **The turn:** the moment the conventional wisdom failed *the author specifically* — placed where the essay pivots from "incomplete" to "the other half."
- **The earned generalization:** move from the specific scar to the principle, so the reader trusts the principle because they saw where it came from.

Borrow narrative technique here, but keep it subordinate to the argument — the lived moment earns the claim, it doesn't replace it.

## Place the sources where they're load-bearing

For each section, note which book and which claim does real work there — so rigor is built into the structure, not bolted on at the end. Don't scatter citations where they don't earn their place; a section with no source isn't a failure if the argument or the lived experience carries it.

## What each outline entry contains

Per section: a one-line **job** (what it must accomplish for the reader), the **evidence or lived moment** it rests on, the **source** (if load-bearing), and a rough **weight**. Plus, up top: the sharpened thesis in one sentence, and a flag if the structure is all abstraction with nowhere for the human to show up.

## The hard boundary

This is a skeleton of intents and placements — **not** prose. Don't write the paragraphs, the opening lines, or the example anecdotes; that's the author's work, and writing it for them is the slop the journal refuses. If a section's job can't be named without inventing the content, that's a sign the idea isn't ready — say so.

## What success looks like

The author can write straight down the outline: every section has a clear job, the thesis is sharp, the lived stake has a deliberate home, the sources are placed where they hold weight, and the shape is honest and not safe. Offer to save the outline into the backlog entry's notes or a working file the author names. When the draft comes back, it's Mercer's (the `line-editor`) to sharpen.
