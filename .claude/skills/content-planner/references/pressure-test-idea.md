# Pressure-Test an Idea

Find out whether a thesis survives contact with its best counterargument — on the page, for free, before it costs the author a month of writing.

## How to test

- **State the thesis back** in one sharp sentence. If it can't be stated sharply, that's the first finding.
- **Argue the strongest opposing case** — not a strawman. The real reason a smart senior engineer would disagree. If the thesis collapses here, the essay would too.
- **Find the "other half."** This journal's whole stance is that most advice is incomplete, not wrong. So is most of its own first-draft thinking. Name what the thesis is currently leaving out — the condition where it fails, the reader it doesn't serve, the case that complicates it. That other half is usually where the essay actually lives.
- **Check it against the books.** Does a source in `{project-root}/reference/` genuinely support the claim, complicate it, or contradict it? Read enough to be sure. A thesis a cited book actually undercuts is a problem to surface now, not after publication.
- **Watch the voice.** If the only thing making the thesis feel fresh is contempt for other writers ("everyone gets this wrong"), flag it — that's the preachy failure mode, and it's a rewrite, not a take. The opposite failure is just as fatal: if the thesis is so hedged and agreeable it offends no one and commits to nothing, it's *safe*, which reads as care-less. Push it toward a sharp position that still feels honest.
- **Run the slop test** (`references/trust-doctrine.md`). Two questions decide whether this is worth trusting: **Is anyone home?** — is there a human center (a real mistake, decision, or scar the author lived), or just a competent summary anyone's pipeline could generate? And **would anything remain an hour after reading?** — a frame, a changed mind, a scar — or is it polished and empty? A thesis with no lived stake isn't dead, but it's not ready: name the missing first-hand angle as the thing the author must supply before writing.

## What success looks like

A verdict, not a hedge: **write it** (and here's the sharper version of the thesis, plus the lived angle to build it around), **reframe it** (the real essay is this adjacent angle), or **park it** (here's the counterargument it can't currently beat, or the missing human center it needs first). The author should come away either more confident in the piece or relieved they didn't spend a month on it. Update the backlog entry to reflect the outcome.
