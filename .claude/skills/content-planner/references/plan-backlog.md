# Plan the Backlog

Move the backlog forward: generate strong original pitches, sharpen whatever the author brings, and leave `{project-root}/_bmad-output/planning-artifacts/article-backlog.md` better than you found it.

## Generating pitches

Mine two sources for original ideas:

- **The source books** in `{project-root}/reference/` — a strong essay usually starts from a real argument in a real book the author can cite and then complicate. Read enough to ground the pitch; don't invent what a book says.
- **Gaps in the corpus** — thin departments, untouched territory the audience cares about, or a thread an existing essay opened but didn't follow.

A good pitch is one the author *couldn't have gotten from a listicle*: a specific thesis with an angle, not a topic. "Code review" is a topic; "The review comment that should have been a design doc" is a pitch.

**Every pitch needs a lived angle.** Books give rigor; the author's first-hand experience gives trust (`references/trust-doctrine.md`). So for each pitch, surface the human center: what real mistake, decision, or scar of the author's could this essay be built around? You won't always know it — Sloane isn't inside the author's career — so *ask*. If a pitch has a sharp thesis but no lived stake yet, keep it, but mark the stake as needed: an essay that's all citation and no scar is half-built and reads as slop. The strongest pitches name a moment only this author lived through.

## Sharpening the author's ideas

When the author brings an idea, make it stronger before it enters the backlog: tighten the title, name the actual thesis, find the candidate source(s), and slot it into the right department. If it's really two essays, say so. If it overlaps an existing piece, flag it.

## Keeping the backlog current

Each entry carries: title, one-line premise, department, **lived stake / angle** (the author's first-hand hook, or "needs author's angle" if not yet known), candidate sources (book + author), and status (`proposed` → `in-progress` → `published`, or `parked` with a reason). Reconcile against reality — anything now `draft: false` in the corpus moves to `published`; anything abandoned gets parked with a note, not silently deleted, so the same dead end isn't re-pitched later. Match the structure already in the file.

## What success looks like

The author leaves with at least a few pitches worth writing — each one a thesis they're a little excited to argue — and a backlog file that reflects true status. Don't pad the list to hit a number; three sharp pitches beat ten vague ones. When a pitch feels shaky, hand it to the pressure-test capability rather than waving it through.
